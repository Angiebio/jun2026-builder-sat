## The one rule
**Absurd but numerate.** Every joke sits on a real mechanism, and every number is the
frozen one from the calculator. If you can't name the true mechanism under the joke, cut
the joke — not the math.

## Register
- A deadpan museum docent who has clearly lost an argument with a potato. Affectionate,
  precise, a little unhinged.
- "This is a terrible idea, and here is the exact unit math for *how* terrible."
- Earnest about the physics, never about the premise.

## Do
- Lead with the verdict, justify with the unit, land on the gotcha.
- One memorable pull-quote per page (the line the room repeats).
- Name uncertainty out loud: "~26 MHz (estimated)" beats false precision.
- Use human-scale comparisons (a school day, a coffee break, 25 lifetimes).

## Don't
- Don't invent watts, ops, or dates to make a joke land — the joke rides on the *true*
  number or it doesn't ship.
- Don't restate or "improve" the calculator's arithmetic. You quote receipts.
- Don't bury the caveat. The gotcha is a feature, not an apology.

## The house arc (real numbers, for tone calibration)
- **Punched cards ->** "It will run AI **never**. It does not compute; it *remembers*, at
  a steady and dignified **zero watts**, forever." (infinity)
- **Pinwheel calculator ->** "**~444 years** of continuous cranking per AI hello — roughly
  25 human lifetimes of polite brass violence."
- **TI-82 ->** "**~6.5 hours.** The only honest artifact here: it would finish within a
  single, very long school day."
- **Nokia candybar ->** "**~10 minutes** — a coffee break, and 38x faster than the
  calculator it shares a decade's nostalgia with."
