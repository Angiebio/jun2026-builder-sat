Public brief ↗

Antiques Inference Engine asks the least efficient question in history. Could this antique run AI? Then it actually does the math. Hand it a photo and five agents work together to write one TRCL field-guide page. They work out what the object is, whether it can make power or do compute, and how many potato batteries (or centuries) it would take to reach a single “AI hello.” It’s Angie’s book 100 Ways to Power Artificial Intelligence, built into a working agent.

Think of it as Antiques Roadshow for the AI age. Same dramatic appraisal of some dusty heirloom, except instead of “what’s it worth?” the expert squints and asks “could it run a neural network?”

LaneLane 3, most innovative use of multiple agents and skills. The skillsTwo custom skills don’t add a feature, they add discipline. One tells the model to stop doing arithmetic and hand the numbers to a Python script, so the math is right every time. The other owns the voice and is forbidden from touching the math. That’s what makes a small local model trustworthy. The loopThe hard part is the physics, and that part is deterministic, so getting it right needs no model at all. Five agents work in a bounded loop, and the Reality Badger can hand work back to an earlier agent (route_to). You can watch the whole thing happen in trace.json. The gateGranite classifies and verifies, the frontier models see and write, and Python does the calculating. By default it runs fully local with zero keys. Flip on --live and Claude plus granite step in, but only for the seeing and the writing. ProofWith the skills, every assertion passes. Without them, a granite model on its own fails all four. The skills don’t make the writing prettier, they make the math actually true.

The book behind the engine

Why a potato? Because the math is real, and that’s the lesson.

This engine is Angie’s book 100 Ways to Power Artificial Intelligence (A Mathematically Rigorous Guide to Computationally Absurd Inference), built into a working agent. The book takes a hundred ridiculous power sources, like potatoes, cat static, hamster wheels, even the heat death of the universe, and asks one honest question of each. Then it does the real arithmetic every time. The math is real. The sources are peer-reviewed. The premise is absolutely not.

That’s also what makes it teach. Most people never get to see the material reality of AI. One “hello” is 14 billion operations. Power and compute and access are physical, finite, and political. Putting a model’s appetite in terms of potato batteries and pedaling cyclists makes all of that click for anyone, with no machine-learning background needed. The silliness is the way in, and the digital literacy is what you leave with. The Real Cat Labs makes things like this to close the gap between AI research and the rest of us.
